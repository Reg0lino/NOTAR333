Comprehensive Study Guide

Foundations of Notary Public Office
  • Commissioning Body: NY Secretary of State.
  • Term of Office: 4 years, with statewide jurisdiction.
  • Eligibility: At least 18 years old; resident of NYS or maintain a place of business in NYS. Commission vacates if a resident moves out of state without retaining a NYS office.
  • Examination Exemptions: Attorneys licensed in NYS; certain Court Clerks of the Unified Court System.
  • Fees: $60 initial application/renewal; $15 examination; $10 for name/address change (waived for marital status change); $10 for duplicate ID.
  • Oath of Office: Must be sworn and notarized for initial application and every renewal.
  • County Clerk Role: Notaries are commissioned in their county of residence. Clerk maintains a record of commission and signature, and can authenticate signatures for out-of-state use. A Certificate of Official Character can be filed in other counties for a $10 fee.
Core Powers and Prohibited Activities

Authorized Powers (Exec. Law §135)

  • Administer oaths and affirmations.
  • Take affidavits and depositions.
  • Receive and certify acknowledgments and proofs.
  • Protest negotiable instruments (75 cents per protest, 10 cents per notice, max 5).

Prohibited Activities & Ethical Conduct

  • Unauthorized Practice of Law: A non-attorney notary CANNOT give legal advice or draft legal documents (wills, deeds, contracts). This is a misdemeanor.
  • Advertising: Ads in languages other than English must include the disclaimer: "I am not an attorney licensed to practice law and may not give legal advice...".
  • Pecuniary Interest: A notary is disqualified if they are a party to or have a direct financial interest in the transaction. Exception: Notaries who are stockholders/employees of a corporation can notarize for that corporation if they are not an individual party to the instrument.
  • Limitations: Notaries CANNOT solemnize marriages.
  • Duty to Officiate: Refusal to administer an oath/affidavit when requested is a misdemeanor.
Identity Verification

Personal appearance of the signer is mandatory for all notarial acts.

For Physical Appearance:

  • Personal knowledge of the signer.
  • Valid government-issued ID with photo, physical description, and signature.
  • At least two current documents bearing the signer's signature.
  • Oath/affirmation of one or two credible witnesses who know the signer and are known to the notary (or can be identified).

For Electronic Appearance (if signer is not personally known):

  • Credential Analysis: Automated third-party process to validate a government-issued ID.
  • Identity Proofing: Third-party process to confirm the signer's identity by reviewing personal info from public/proprietary data sources (must meet NIST IAL2 standard).
Notarial Certificates & Acts
  • Required Information (Exec. Law §137): Below the signature, in black ink, the notary must affix their name, "Notary Public State of New York", county of original qualification, and commission expiration date.
  • NYC Requirement: Notaries in NYC must also affix their official number(s).
  • Validity of Acts (Exec. Law §142-a): Acts are generally NOT invalidated by minor, unknown defects (e.g., expired term, acting outside jurisdiction) unless the defect was known or apparent on the certificate.
  • Willful Failure to Comply: While the act itself may not be invalid, willful failure to include required info can lead to disciplinary action.
Electronic Notarization (REN)

Governed by Exec. Law §135-c and 19 NYCRR Part 182, effective Jan 25, 2023.

  • Registration: Notaries must register their capability with the Secretary of State.
  • Notary Location: Must be physically located within NYS at the time of notarization.
  • Signer Location: Can be anywhere (with special conditions if outside the U.S.).
  • Technology: Must use secure audio-video communication. Notary must maintain sole control of their electronic signature.
  • Recording: Must create and retain an audio-visual recording of every REN for 10 years.
  • Fee: Up to $25 per electronic notarial act.
  • RIN Prohibited: Remote Ink Notarization is NOT permitted after Jan 31, 2023.
Mandatory Notary Journal

Effective Jan 25, 2023, for ALL notaries and ALL notarial acts.

  • Retention Period: Must be kept for 10 years.
  • Required Entries:
    • Date and approximate time of the act.
    • Type of notarial act.
    • Full name and address of individuals involved.
    • Number and type of services provided.
    • Type of credential used for identification.
    • Verification procedures used.
    • For REN: Communication technology and providers used.
  • Proposed (S.398): For residential real property conveyances, proposed rules include adding an inked thumbprint and a copy of the ID to the journal. This is NOT current law.
Violations, Misconduct & Penalties
  • Class D Felony (up to 7 years): Forgery in the second degree (e.g., falsely altering a deed).
  • Class E Felony (up to 4 years): Issuing a false certificate with intent to defraud.
  • Class A Misdemeanor (up to 1 year): Official misconduct.
  • Misdemeanor: Unauthorized practice of law; acting without an appointment; acting before filing oath of office; refusal to officiate when requested.
  • Disciplinary Action: Secretary of State can suspend or remove a notary for misconduct, fraud, or misstatement on application after a hearing.

Glossary of Terms

Acknowledgment

A formal declaration before an authorized public officer by a person who has executed an instrument, that such execution is their act and deed.

Affiant

A person who makes and subscribes an affidavit.

Affidavit

A written statement sworn to or affirmed before an officer authorized to administer an oath or affirmation.

Affirmation

A solemn declaration equivalent to an oath, made by a person who conscientiously declines taking an oath.

Apostille

A certificate issued by the Secretary of State that authenticates the signature of a public official on a document for use in foreign countries that are parties to the Hague Apostille Convention.

Conveyance (Deed)

A written instrument by which title to real property is transferred from one party to another.

Deponent

A person who gives testimony under oath or affirmation, particularly in a deposition.

Deposition

The testimony of a witness taken out of court under oath or affirmation, usually in writing, under a commission or before a notary public.

Electronic Notarial Act

A notarial act performed by an electronic notary public using communication technology.

Jurat

The clause in an affidavit stating when, where, and before whom it was sworn to or affirmed.

Oath

A solemn declaration, accompanied by a swearing to God or a revered person or thing, that one's statement is true or that one will be bound to a promise.

Perjury

The willful giving of false testimony under oath or affirmation on a material matter.

Proof of Execution

The formal declaration of a subscribing witness to the execution of an instrument.

Protest

A formal statement in writing by a notary public, at the request of the holder of a bill or note, of its presentment for payment or acceptance and refusal thereof.

Seal

While not required in New York State, if a notary uses a seal, it must clearly identify the notary.

Venue

The geographical location where a notarial act is performed, typically indicated by 'State of New York, County of [County Name]'.

Will

A legal document for property distribution at death. Notaries are cautioned not to execute an acknowledgment of a will's execution as it's not a valid attestation.
